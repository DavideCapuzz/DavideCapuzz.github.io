Robo-Magellan is a robotics competition that pushes autonomous robots to navigate real-world outdoor environments using GPS, sensors, and intelligent programming — all without any human control once the run begins.

The goal? Guide the robot to a designated target location while avoiding real-world hazards such as trees, curbs, poles, unpredictable lighting conditions, and uneven terrain. Success requires both durable hardware and sophisticated decision-making software.

THe Robot platform - Wheele

When I moved to Peoria, IL, I joined the Central Illinois Robotics Club, a group of friends bound by a shared passion for robotics and competitions. Years ago, the club had built Wheele, an autonomous platform designed specifically for Robo-Magellan challenges. Back then, Wheele was powered by Arduinos and Raspberry Pi boards, running ROS 1 as its main control framework.

CIRC logo

Over time, the technology aged, and the team agreed it was time for a major upgrade. I saw this as the perfect opportunity to take the project to the next level — replacing outdated components, enhancing sensing capabilities, and migrating the entire software architecture to ROS 2.

Originally launched by the Seattle Robotics Society, the Robo-Magellan competition is a unique blend of mechanical engineering, sensor fusion, and AI-driven decision-making. Robots start at a fixed location, use GPS to navigate toward a target waypoint, and must adapt on the fly to obstacles detected via cameras, ultrasonic sensors, LiDAR, or other range-finding devices.

For Wheele, the specific challenge is to navigate through an unknown outdoor environment and locate multiple traffic cones scattered within a designated competition zone. On paper, the task sounds straightforward — “just drive to the cones.” In reality, building a robust, competition-ready system requires:

  • Precise GPS integration to maintain accuracy even in areas with signal noise or multipath interference.
  • Sensor fusion algorithms to merge data from LiDAR, vision, and ultrasonic sensors into a reliable world model.
  • Obstacle detection and avoidance capable of handling both static and dynamic hazards.
  • Robust path-planning to find efficient, collision-free routes in a space the robot has never seen before.
  • High-level decision-making for cone detection, verification, and sequencing.

Migrating to ROS 2 not only modernized Wheele’s software stack but also unlocked advanced features like improved real-time performance, better communication between distributed nodes, and greater modularity for future upgrades.

The journey of upgrading Wheele has been as challenging as the competition itself — a mix of electronics design, embedded programming, control theory, and field testing in all sorts of unpredictable Illinois weather. But that’s the essence of Robo-Magellan: pushing both the robot and the team behind it to perform at their best in the real world.
